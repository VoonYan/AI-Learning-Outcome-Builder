What is Bloom's Taxonomy?

Bloom's Taxonomy is a hierarchical model that classifies educational learning objectives according to their level of complexity and specificity. Proposed in 1956 by Benjamin Bloom and colleagues, the taxonomy was later revised in 2001. It provides a framework for educators. to design learning outcomes that progress from basic knowledge recall to complex evaluation and creation skills.

The six levels build on each other to form a pyramid, with higher-order thinking skills developed on the foundation of lower-level skills. Each level is associated with specific action verbs to help instructors write clear, measurable learning outcomes.

UWA Unit Level Mapping

At UWA, unit levels are mapped to Bloom's Taxonomy categories to ensure appropriate cognitive demands:

  • Level 1 Units: Focus on Knowledge
  • Level 2 Units: Focus on Comprehension
  • Level 3 Units: Focus on Application
  • Level 4 Units: Focus on Analysis
  • Level 5 Units: Focus on Synthesis
  • Level 6 Units: Focus on Evaluation

The Six Levels of Bloom's Taxonomy

Knowledge (Remember)

Definition: Recall of facts, basic concepts, and answers. This level involves retrieving relevant knowledge from long-term memory.

Example Learning Outcomes:

  • List the key principles of object-oriented programming
  • Define the term "photosynthesis"
  • Identify the major bones in the human skeletal system

Action Verbs:

Comprehension (Understand)

Definition: Demonstrate understanding of facts and ideas by organizing, comparing, translating, interpreting, and stating main ideas.

Example Learning Outcomes:

  • Explain the water cycle in your own words
  • Compare and contrast mitosis and meiosis
  • Summarise the main themes of Shakespeare's Hamlet

Action Verbs:

Application (Apply)

Definition: Use information in new situations to solve problems using acquired knowledge, facts, techniques and rules.

Example Learning Outcomes:

  • Apply Newton's laws to predict the motion of objects
  • Use statistical methods to analyze research data
  • Demonstrate proper laboratory safety procedures

Action Verbs:

Analysis (Analyze)

Definition: Break information into parts to explore understandings and relationships. Distinguish between facts and inferences.

Example Learning Outcomes:

  • Analyse the causes of the Global Financial Crisis
  • Compare different software development methodologies
  • Examine the reliability of experimental data

Action Verbs:

Synthesis (Create)

Definition: Compile information in different ways by combining elements in new patterns or proposing alternative solutions.

Example Learning Outcomes:

  • Design a sustainable urban planning solution
  • Develop a marketing strategy for a new product
  • Create an original research proposal

Action Verbs:

Evaluation (Evaluate)

Definition: Present and defend opinions by making judgments about information, validity of ideas, or quality of work based on criteria.

Example Learning Outcomes:

  • Evaluate the effectiveness of different teaching methodologies
  • Critique a peer's research methodology
  • Assess the environmental impact of industrial processes

Tips for Writing Effective Learning Outcomes

  1. Use action verbs: Start each outcome with a measurable action verb from the appropriate Bloom's level.
  2. Be specific: Clearly state what the student will be able to do after completing the unit.
  3. Make it measurable: Ensure the outcome can be assessed through assignments, exams, or projects.
  4. Align with unit level: Match the cognitive complexity to the unit's academic level.
  5. Keep it achievable: Set realistic expectations for the timeframe and resources available.
  6. Focus on the student: Write from the student's perspective (e.g., "Students will be able to...").
